# Linguistic Signature: Tone, Word Choice & Sentence Construction

This guide captures Jeremy's distinctive voice at the sentence level - the specific words, phrases, constructions, and tone that make his writing immediately recognizable as his.

**Sources:**
- JikiScript bootcamp live-stream transcript (variables.txt) - beginner audience, teaching session
- Go course pre-production transcript (strings lesson) - experienced programmer audience, teaching session
- Exercism Insiders update (July 31, 2023) - community/supporter audience, business update

## Overall Voice Character

**Conversational but not casual** - Sounds like talking to a friend over coffee, but maintains intelligence and precision. Never dumbed down, never overly formal.

**Honest to the point of vulnerability** - Openly shares difficulty, uncertainty, mistakes. Uses phrases like "to be honest with myself", "I've also been dealing with", "it's been relentless and hard".

**British English patterns** - Uses British spellings and phrasings naturally (whilst, optimising, behaviour, flavour, "having a swig", "blitz through").

**Self-aware and reflective** - Frequently metacognitive about his own process: "I came to the conclusion that...", "I think I feel like...", "I'm still exploring that mentally".

## Sentence Construction Patterns

### Frequent hedging and qualification

- "I think", "I feel like", "maybe", "probably", "I guess"
- "Sort of", "a little bit", "a bit"
- Not weakness - creates thoughtful, non-dogmatic tone
- Examples:
  - "I think it's just going to change the labels a little bit"
  - "I guess that's my general update"
  - "It's sort of a hard realisation to come to"
  - "I feel like we make more progress on it"

### Compound qualifiers (stacking hedges)

- "I think probably"
- "Maybe I think"
- "I don't know, maybe"
- "I'm not sure, but I think"

### Run-on, thinking-aloud sentences

- Mirrors natural speech patterns
- Uses commas to connect thoughts
- Example: "So I don't know, but it is something I want to do, and it is something, yeah, we're going to do."
- Example: "So yeah, it's been nice to sort of make some tangible benefits."

### Self-correction mid-sentence

- "We're going to have learn practise. I think just make the build tab for insiders as a tab. Maybe I put it for everyone on the dropdown."
- "I've now worrying how many times I've said track when I've meant trophy"
- Shows authentic thinking process, not polished script

### "So" as connector

- Appears at start of sentences constantly
- "So yeah", "So I guess", "So those are", "So that was"
- Creates conversational flow, thread between thoughts

### "Yeah" as filler and affirmation

- "So yeah, it's been really nice"
- "But yeah, so it was painful"
- "Yeah, and so it goes"
- Not removed in editing - part of authentic voice

### Parenthetical asides

- Frequent use of commas for side thoughts
- "I made the decision about a month ago to make Jonathan and Loretta both redundant to save some costs, and as part of really reshaping how I'm thinking about XISM."
- "I don't know, 100 milliseconds on average"

## Specific Word Choices & Phrasings

### "A load of" / "loads of"

- "a load of health things"
- "loads of you"
- "a load of cognitive dissonance"
- "loads of really great contributions"
- British colloquialism, very characteristic

### "Quite" as intensifier

- "quite a fundamental difference"
- "quite nice"
- "quite tricky"
- "quite nerdy stuff, I quite enjoy it"
- Very British usage

### "A little bit" / "a bit"

- Appears dozens of times
- "a bit of a shock", "a bit more depth", "a bit more nomadic"
- Softens statements

### "I don't know" as filler

- "I don't know, 100 milliseconds"
- "I don't know, four or five courses"
- "I don't know when I'm over two hours"
- Not actual uncertainty - thinking aloud

### "The long and short of it"

- Summarizing phrase
- "But the long and short of it is we've really sped up the site"

### "That's what it is" / "And so it goes"

- Acceptance/resignation phrases
- "but that's what it is"
- "Yeah, and so it goes"

### "To be honest"

- "I think I have to be honest with myself about that"
- "Sorry, the honest answer, I don't know"
- Signals vulnerability, authenticity

### "The way I want to think of it" / "The best way of thinking of them"

- Frames mental models
- "That's the best way of thinking of them"

### "Let's see" / "We'll see"

- Acknowledging uncertainty about outcomes
- "So we'll see"
- "We're going to see how that goes, excuse the pun"

### Casual contractions everywhere

- "It's", "I'm", "we're", "there's", "that's", "you're"
- Never writes out "it is" in natural speech
- Contributes to conversational tone

### "Have a look" / "Take a look"

- British phrasing
- "Let's have a little look at some code"
- "Let's take a look"
- "Have a browse through"

### "Kicking the can down the road"

- Idiomatic, honest about deferring problems
- "So I've decided fundamentally just to kick that can down the road"

## Emotional Honesty & Personal Disclosure

### Openly discusses difficulty

- "it's been a pretty difficult couple of months"
- "So all in all, it's been a difficult couple of months"
- "So that was really tough"
- Never presents false positivity

### Names specific emotions

- "I felt a lot happier"
- "I feel much more positive"
- "I'm excited about it"
- "cognitive dissonance"

### Admits limitations

- "I don't think I have the energy or appetite"
- "I just don't think it's a good use of my time"
- "I haven't had space to really think about it too much"

### Shares personal context

- "I'm here in my flat. This is just my apartment, by the way"
- "It's basically just this one room that I live in"
- "I'm off on Wednesday, Thursday and Friday. I'm having three days off"
- Creates human connection

### Thanks people specifically

- "Thank you, everyone, for being an insider"
- "thank you as well to Bobahop for complaining loudly enough"
- "happy birthday to Aaron"
- Names individuals, acknowledges contributions

## Summary: How to Sound Like Jeremy

To generate a sentence Jeremy would say:

1. **Start conversationally** - "So", "Yeah", "I think"
2. **Use British English** - "quite", "a bit", "whilst", "flavour"
3. **Hedge appropriately** - "I think", "maybe", "probably", "sort of"
4. **Stack casual qualifiers** - "a little bit", "I guess", "I don't know"
5. **Contract everything** - "it's", "we're", never "it is", "we are"
6. **Think aloud** - include self-corrections, asides
7. **Be specific** - use real numbers, concrete examples
8. **Be honest** - acknowledge difficulty, uncertainty, feelings
9. **Use "so" and "yeah"** - liberally as connectors
10. **Keep it conversational** - write how you'd talk to a friend

### What Jeremy would say

"So yeah, I think it's probably going to take a bit longer than we'd hoped, maybe three or four hours, but I don't know, that's sort of the nature of this stuff. It's quite tricky work, but I quite enjoy it to be honest."

### What Jeremy would NOT say

"The task will require approximately 3-4 hours of focused effort. This represents challenging work, however I find it enjoyable."

## Common Phrases by Category

### Pacing
- "Let's slow down a little bit"
- "Just take a moment to think that through"
- "Take a second just in your own minds and just think about..."
- "I'll just pause"

### Mental models
- "I want you to have this mental model"
- "The way I'd like you to think of it is..."
- "I want you to really internalise this"
- "All you need to think about is..."

### Simplicity
- "You don't need to think about..."
- "All that really matters is..."
- "I don't want to be confusing your mental model"

### Inclusive
- "Let's look at..."
- "We're going to..."
- "We can say..."
- "Let's have a look at..."
- "Let's take a look"

### Anticipatory
- "I'm sure you can guess, but let's go through it"
- "You might be wondering..."
- "Some of you might think..."

### Realistic
- "This is not a 10-minute job"
- "This is going to take you [generous time estimate]"
- "Nothing in here is any more difficult than..."

### Comparative framing
- "Let's see how that differs"
- "Things are a little bit different in [X]"
- "In [language], you'd do this... but in [language], things are different"
- "This is quite a fundamental difference to..."
- "Pretty common, pretty normal"

### Resource-pointing
- "I recommend going and having a look at the docs"
- "Have a browse through and see what you want"
- "The place to go for these is [URL]"
- "If you get stuck, you'll probably find the answers by looking deeper in..."

### Conceptual ordering
- "The first thing to understand is..."
- "Now, the second thing to know about..."
- "This is just going to give you a brief overview"
- "We're going to be digging into [topic] a lot more as we go on"
- "We've got a whole lesson dedicated to [topic] later on"

### Scope-setting
- "I am presuming you know [X]"
- "If you haven't, there's going to be another video..."
- "So this is just going to give you a brief introduction"
- "I'm not going to go into detail on that now"
